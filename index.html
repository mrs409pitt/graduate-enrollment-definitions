The Graduate Enrollment Funnel

The graduate enrollment funnel represents the complete journey from first awareness through matriculation. Understanding each stage and the conversion points between them is essential for managing enrollment effectively.

1
Awareness

Prospective students first become aware of your institution and graduate programs.

2
Consider

Prospects actively research your programs and seek detailed information.

3
Engage

Deeper exploration of specific programs and building relationships.

4
Decision

Serious evaluation of your programs against alternatives.

5
Applying

Actively working on or submitting applications.

6
Admissions

Applications under review and admission decisions being made.

7
Decision/Offer

Admitted students evaluating offers and making final decisions.

8
Accepts/Deposits

Students accept offers and submit enrollment deposits.

9
Enroll

Complete enrollment requirements and officially matriculate.

üí° Why Small Improvements = Big Results

Improving inquiry‚Üíapplication from 15% to 17% (just 2 percentage points) yields 20 additional applicants per 1,000 inquiries. Across 10 programs, that's potentially 10 more enrolled students - significant revenue impact!

Graduate Recruitment & Enrollment Strategy Framework

The graduate enrollment lifecycle can be organized into four strategic phases, each with distinct objectives and stakeholder responsibilities. Understanding these phases helps align team efforts and optimize resource allocation.

1

Recruitment & Marketing

Stages Covered:

Awareness ‚Üí Consider

Area of Focus

Student Focus:

  • Student Mindset: "I need a graduate degree for my career goals" / "What programs exist in my field?"
  • Student Actions: Searching online, clicking ads, visiting websites, attending fairs

Staff Focus:

  • Develop and execute brand awareness campaigns
  • Manage digital advertising
  • Attend fairs and events
  • Create content
  • Drive web traffic (SEO/SEM)

Best Practices

Primary Tactics:

  • Digital advertising (Google, LinkedIn, social media)
  • Search engine optimization (SEO)
  • Content marketing (blogs, program pages)
  • Graduate school fairs and events
  • Partner organizations and employers

Success Indicators:

  • Website traffic volume
  • Social media reach and engagement
  • Event attendance
  • Brand awareness metrics

Primary Roles: Marketing Team, Recruitment Officers

2

Enrollment Nurturing

Stages Covered:

Engage ‚Üí Decision

Area of Focus

Student Focus:

  • Student Mindset: "I want to learn more about this specific program" / "How does this fit my career goals?"
  • Student Actions: Downloading materials, attending webinars, requesting counselor meetings, exploring curriculum

Staff Focus:

  • Follow up with inquiries
  • Provide program information
  • Answer questions
  • Nurture relationships
  • Encourage applications

Best Practices

Primary Tactics:

  • Personalized email campaigns
  • One-on-one counselor consultations
  • Information sessions and webinars
  • Virtual campus tours
  • Student and alumni testimonials
  • Faculty connections

Success Indicators:

  • Inquiry volume and rate
  • Email engagement rates
  • Webinar attendance
  • Inquiry-to-application conversion

Primary Roles: Recruitment Officers, Admissions Counselors

3

Admissions

Stages Covered:

Applying ‚Üí Admissions ‚Üí Decision/Offer

Area of Focus

Student Focus:

  • Student Mindset: "I'm ready to apply" / "What materials do I need?" / "Was I admitted?"
  • Student Actions: Starting applications, gathering materials (transcripts, recommendations), submitting applications, waiting for decisions

Staff Focus:

  • Assist with application submission
  • Provide technical support
  • Evaluate applications
  • Conduct interviews (if applicable)
  • Make admission decisions
  • Assemble financial aid packages

Best Practices

Primary Tactics:

  • Application portal guidance
  • Progress reminders and nudges
  • Requirements checklists
  • Deadline reminders
  • Decision letters with clear next steps
  • Personalized financial aid packages

Success Indicators:

  • Application starts and submission volume
  • Application completion rate
  • Decision turnaround time
  • Admit rate

Primary Roles: Admissions Counselors, Application Processors, Admissions Committee, Financial Aid Officers

4

Yield & Melt Prevention

Stages Covered:

Accepts/Deposits ‚Üí Enroll

Area of Focus

Student Focus:

  • Student Mindset: "Should I accept this offer?" / "What do I need to do to complete enrollment?" / "I'm excited but nervous"
  • Student Actions: Comparing offers, accepting admission, submitting deposit, completing enrollment forms, registering for orientation and classes

Staff Focus:

  • Yield communications
  • Facilitate enrollment completion
  • Coordinate orientation
  • Assist with course selection and registration
  • Remove barriers to enrollment
  • Prevent "summer melt"

Best Practices

Primary Tactics:

  • Admitted student webinars/events
  • Faculty and student connections
  • Campus visit days
  • Clear enrollment checklists
  • Orientation scheduling
  • Welcome communications
  • Pre-orientation programs

Success Indicators:

  • Offer-to-deposit yield rate
  • Deposit-to-enrollment rate (melt rate)
  • Enrollment completion timeline
  • Orientation attendance

Primary Roles: Enrollment Managers, Enrollment Coordinators, Academic Advisors, Registrar

üéØ Strategic Alignment

These four phases represent the complete enrollment lifecycle. Each phase requires different strategies, metrics, and team collaboration. Understanding which phase a prospect is in helps you deliver the right message at the right time with the appropriate level of support.

Phase Transitions: Critical Handoff Points

Phase 1 ‚Üí 2

Consider ‚Üí Engage: When a prospect submits an RFI, they transition from marketing to active recruitment nurturing. This is where personalized communication begins.

Phase 2 ‚Üí 3

Decision ‚Üí Applying: When a prospect starts an application, they move from enrollment nurturing to admissions processing. Application support becomes critical.

Phase 3 ‚Üí 4

Decision/Offer ‚Üí Accepts: When an admitted student deposits, they transition from admissions to yield management and enrollment services. Preventing melt is now the priority.

6 Cross-Funnel KPIs That Matter Most

These six metrics span multiple funnel stages and provide the most comprehensive view of enrollment performance.

1. Funnel Conversion Rate

The percentage of prospects who advance from one funnel stage to the next.

(Number Advanced √∑ Number in Stage) √ó 100

Benchmarks: Inquiry‚ÜíApplication: 13-18% | App Completion: 50-60% | Offer‚ÜíDeposit: 60-70%

2. Days Between Stages

The average number of days for a prospect to move from one stage to the next (Pipeline Velocity).

Average (Date Next Stage - Date Current Stage)

Targets: Inquiry‚ÜíApp: ‚â§30 days | App‚ÜíDecision: <14 days

3. Cost per Stage Transition

Total marketing and recruitment expenditure required to move one student from one stage to the next.

Total Stage Spend √∑ Number of Transitions

Benchmarks: Cost/Inquiry: $140 avg | Cost/Enrolled: $2,850 avg

4. LTV:CAC Ratio

The ratio of Lifetime Value (total revenue from a student) to Customer Acquisition Cost.

Lifetime Value √∑ Customer Acquisition Cost

Targets: Minimum: 3:1 | Healthy: 5:1 | Excellent: 7:1+

5. Lead Quality by Channel

How effectively each marketing channel generates leads that convert deeper into the funnel and ultimately enroll.

(Conversion Rate √ó Volume) √∑ Cost

Track: Inquiry volume, conversion rates, cost per enrolled by channel

6. Source-to-Enrollment Attribution

Full-funnel tracking connecting each enrolled student back to their original marketing source and all touchpoints.

UTM tracking + multi-touch attribution

Models: First-touch, Last-touch, Multi-touch, Time-decay

A Salesforce record representing a potential student who has expressed interest but has not yet been qualified or converted into a Contact with an Opportunity. When qualified, Lead converts to Contact + Opportunity.

A measure of how effectively each marketing channel generates leads that convert deeper into the funnel and ultimately enroll. One of the 6 critical cross-funnel KPIs.

M

Opportunity (Salesforce)

A Salesforce record used to track the recruitment and application pipeline from a prospective student through to matriculation. Typically one opportunity per program application (student may have multiple Opportunities for different programs).

The stages an opportunity moves through in Salesforce, typically: Prospective ‚Üí In-Progress Application ‚Üí Application ‚Üí Admit ‚Üí Deposit ‚Üí Enrolled.

Marketing Cloud's AI-powered tool that uses data analytics and machine learning to predict student behaviors, optimize send times, recommend content, and improve recruitment outcomes.

Prospect

A potential student in the early awareness stage who has not yet submitted an inquiry or expressed formal interest.

R

A structured query and display of Salesforce data, used to analyze recruitment and enrollment performance.

Response Time

The time it takes for staff to respond to a prospect inquiry or question. Target: <24 hours (ideal: <4 hours).

RFI (Request for Information)

A form submitted by a prospective student to request more information about a program. Creates an inquiry record in the CRM.

Rolling Admission

An admission policy where applications are reviewed as they are received, and decisions are made on an ongoing basis rather than on specific decision dates.

S

Salesforce

A cloud-based Customer Relationship Management (CRM) platform used to manage student data, track interactions, manage recruitment pipelines, and execute marketing campaigns. The primary CRM at the University of Pittsburgh.

Scholarship

Financial aid awarded to students based on merit, need, or other criteria. Does not need to be repaid.

Full-funnel tracking that connects each enrolled student back to their original marketing source and tracks all touchpoints along their journey. One of the 6 critical cross-funnel KPIs.

Individuals in Marketing Cloud who have opted in to receive communications through email, SMS, or other channels.

A conversion rate calculated based on qualified prospects only, excluding unqualified inquiries.

The time between when an action is initiated and when it's completed (e.g., from application submission to admission decision).

The number of visits to your website within a given timeframe. A session ends after 30 minutes of inactivity or at midnight.

Simple Implementation Rules

To avoid problems later, follow these three critical rules:

1

Never change how someone originally found you

When a person fills out a form, you capture where they came from (for example: Google, an ad, an email, or another website). That original information should be saved exactly as it came in and never edited, even if you later decide to label or interpret it differently.

üí° Think of this as the receipt. You don't rewrite receipts.

2

It's okay to change your interpretation of the data

How you credit a lead (first click, last click, best click, etc.) can change over time as your reporting improves. Those results should be easy to update or recalculate without touching the original source information.

üí° The facts stay the same; your analysis can evolve.

3

Use the same labels everywhere from day one

Decide early what words you'll use for things like:

  • Lead status (New, Working, Enrolled)
  • Marketing channels (Paid Search, Paid Social, Email)
  • Consent status (Opted In, Opted Out)

If everyone uses the same labels consistently, reports stay clean. If not, you'll spend months fixing data instead of learning from it.

üí° Agree on the vocabulary before you start writing the story.

‚úÖ Best Practice Summary

Start with primary fields only. Add secondary fields as your reporting matures. Most importantly: never edit raw source data and use consistent labels from day one. These two practices will save you countless hours of data cleanup later.
